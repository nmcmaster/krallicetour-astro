⏺ The two files are an interesting pair — bigger gap than expected:

  - porous_4_break.mp3: 14 MB, 6 min 9 sec, 320 kbps stereo
  - porous_3_intro.mp3: 450 KB, 11.4 sec, 320 kbps stereo

  Is 14 MB a problem? Mostly no, partly yes.

  - Initial page load: unaffected. preload="metadata" only fetches the MP3
  header and seek table — tens of KB. Visitors who never press play never
  download the audio.
  - On click: progressive streaming. The browser starts playback within ~1
  second on broadband; the rest streams while you listen. So it's not a
  wall-of-silence experience.
  - Where it hurts: mobile data and slow connections — 14 MB is ~10× what a
  60-90s loop at sensible bitrate would be. And anyone who lets the site play
  through cycles a full 14 MB per loop iteration. Also worth noting: at 6 min 9
  sec, it's not really a "loop" in the sense we originally discussed — <audio 
  loop> will still repeat it, but the seamless ambient-sampler feel comes from a
   tight 60-90s clip.

  Two cheap wins, in priority order:
  1. Re-encode at 160 kbps (or 128) — ffmpeg -i porous_4_break.mp3 -b:a 160k 
  out.mp3. Cuts the file to ~7 MB (or ~5.6 MB at 128). Imperceptible quality
  difference for web ambient delivery.

⏺ Actually the math works out better than your gut suggests, because of how
  browsers handle <audio loop>:

  A loop in a single tab = one download, then free repetition. The browser
  buffers the file once and replays from memory; no new request on each loop
  iteration. So a visitor who hits play and lets it run for 8 hours costs you
  exactly one file download, not 200.

  The real cost is track switches and fresh visitors. With 7 trimmed loops at
  ~1.5 MB each (90s @ 128 kbps), the worst-case visitor — one who clicks through
   all 7 tracks once — costs ~10 MB. Then:

  - 100 GB / 10 MB = ~10,000 fresh "listen to everything" visits per month
  - Most visitors won't click through all 7 — realistic average is probably 2-3
  tracks, so 25-30k+ visits is plausible
  - Repeat visits within ~24h hit the browser HTTP cache (0 cost)

  Even with the current unoptimized 14 MB file, you'd get ~7,000 plays before
  the cap. For a tour-announcement page driven by social posts and word of
  mouth, that's a comfortable margin.

  Realistic risks worth knowing:
  - Going viral on Reddit/Twitter — a single front-page hit can do 50k+
  visitors. Unlikely but not impossible for a Krallice tour drop.
  - Link unfurl bots and audio-sniffing crawlers occasionally fetch mp3s.
  Usually negligible volume.

  Cheap insurance if you want it:
  1. Stay on track for the trimmed re-encodes (it's the single biggest win)
  2. If usage gets serious, Netlify Pro is $19/mo for 1 TB — at that point
  you're handling ~100k full plays
  3. Or offload audio specifically to Bunny CDN (~$0.01/GB) or Cloudflare R2
  (egress-free) — leave the HTML on Netlify. The player code wouldn't change
  beyond making file accept absolute URLs.

  For now: ship it. You'll see real numbers in the Netlify dashboard within a
  day or two, and we can decide whether to optimize based on actual traffic
  instead of guessing.

❯ what will happen if i hit the limit? will the page go dark or will netlify    
  just prompt me to go pro?                                                     

⏺ Honest answer: Netlify's policy has shifted over the years, so verify in your
  dashboard, but the general pattern on the free tier is warn, then 
  throttle/suspend — not silently bill you. You'll get emails at ~75% and ~100%
  usage, and if you actually blow past 100% they typically either (a) serve a
  "bandwidth exceeded" placeholder page until next billing cycle, or (b) prompt
  you to upgrade and resume service once you do. They don't auto-bill overages
  on the free tier without your consent — that's reserved for paid plans where
  you've opted into pay-as-you-go.

  Practical advice: turn on the usage notifications in your team settings, and
  if you're worried about a sudden viral moment while you're asleep, add a
  payment method with a low overage cap — that way the site stays up but you
  can't get surprised by a four-figure bill.
